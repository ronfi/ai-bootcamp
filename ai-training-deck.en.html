01 · A Real Revolution · Putting AI on history's map

The fourth time around: every revolution has reshuffled the ranks of great powers

Ⅰ

Steam Revolution 1760s–

Steam engines · Textile machines · Railways

Ⅱ

Electric Revolution 1870s–

Electricity · Internal combustion · Assembly lines

Energy and manufacturing at scale — modern industry takes shape

Front-runners: the US and Germany — new powers rise, Britain's hegemony loosens

Ⅲ

Information Revolution 1950s–

Computers · The internet · Smartphones

Machines take over computing and moving information

Front-runner: the US locks in tech supremacy; China catches the last train and pulls even at the application layer

Ⅳ

Intelligence Revolution 2020s · happening now

Large models · Agents · Robots

For the first time, machines replace the human brain — cognition, judgment, creativity

Front-runners: the US and China, neck and neck — exactly why both have bet their national destiny on it

Two things are different this time: the first three revolutions replaced muscle and hands — this one replaces the brain itself, so there is no ceiling on its reach; and the first three took decades to spread, while this one spreads in months — see the next slide.

03 · The Battle of Models · Fundamentals I: What is AI

What a large model really is: a giant function that learned to finish sentences

It doesn't look up answers — it computes probabilities. Every bit of "intelligence" you see comes from predicting the next word, over and over again.

Parameters = a trillion knobs

A model is a vast mathematical network, and its "parameters" are the adjustable knobs inside — frontier models have over a trillion. Training is simply turning every knob to the right position.

Tokens = the building blocks of text

Models chop text into small chunks for processing (the Chinese word for "artificial intelligence" ≈ 2 tokens). APIs bill by the token — that's where the "per million tokens" on the pricing slide comes from.

Emergence = quantity becomes quality

Scaling laws: grow parameters, data, and compute together, and abilities like understanding, reasoning, and creativity "grow out on their own" — which is exactly why the whole world is scrambling for compute.

In one sentence: AI isn't "programmed" into being — it's "fed" into being. From oceans of text, it learned the patterns of language, and of the world itself.
03 · The Battle of Models · Fundamentals II: How training works

"Forging" a large model: first read the entire internet, then teach it to behave

① Pre-training

Read trillions of tokens of text (≈ a few million libraries), learning the patterns of language and the world through a guess-the-next-word game. This is where the compute and money get burned.

② Post-training

Human demonstrations + ratings (RLHF and friends) teach it to be helpful, honest, and well-behaved — from "can chat" to "can work."

③ Inference (usage)

Every question you ask is one "inference," billed by the token. Train once, serve hundreds of millions.

Agents: AI is no longer just chat

From "here's an answer" to "the task is done" — agents are the biggest paradigm shift of 2025–2026. Claude Code is our main case study, followed by two newer faces.

05 · Agents

Three leaps in three years: completion → conversation → agents

2021 · Completion

GitHub Copilot: you write half a line, it guesses the next one.

2022 · Conversation

ChatGPT: you ask, it answers — the copy-pasting is still on you.

2025 · Agents

Claude Code: give it a goal, and it does the work itself.

Claude Code's working loop in the terminal

① Understand

Reads the codebase and docs on its own to figure out the current state.

② Plan

Breaks the task down, lays out an approach, asks before any major action.

③ Execute

Writes code, edits files, runs commands, installs dependencies — many steps, back to back.

④ Verify

Runs the tests itself, reads the errors, and keeps fixing until they pass.

The key difference: when something breaks, it doesn't dump the error on you — it reads the error, fixes the code, and tries again. That is the line between a "tool" and a "colleague."

What this means for ordinary people

  • The coding barrier is gone; engineering judgment isn't. AI writes the code for you — it doesn't decide what to build, what good looks like, or whether it's right.
  • What gets replaced isn't "engineers" — it's people who can't use AI. Decomposing problems, specifying precisely, and verifying results are the new core competencies.
  • Question quality = output quality. Vague asks get vague results; spell out the goal, the constraints, and the acceptance criteria, and the output changes immediately.
  • Ordinary people carry almost no "experience deficit." This way of working only appeared in 2025 and only went mainstream in 2026 — you and a ten-year veteran are standing at nearly the same starting line. That almost never happens in history.

Miles logged on the old track don't carry over to the new one — the road ahead is the same length for both.

Hands-on exercises

Five exercises, all driven by the same framework:

Goal

State in one sentence what you're going to produce

Tool

Which AI to use, and how to get started

Deliverable

What you hand over for review when you're done

Review

Which prompt mattered most? Where did the AI go wrong?

Exercise 1 · Data infrastructure

Have an agent build a "scrape → clean → store" data pipeline (market data / news / weather — pick one) that runs repeatably.
Deliverable: a working script + one clean data file.

Exercise 2 · Investment analysis

Pick an AI company from today's talk (Zhipu / MiniMax / NVIDIA…), have the AI verify the data online, then produce a one-page investment brief: business, valuation, risks.
Deliverable: a shareable one-page web page or document.

Exercise 3 · Video transcription

Hand the AI a talk or lecture video: transcribe it → turn it into structured notes with subheadings and standout quotes.
Deliverable: notes polished enough to publish as-is.

Exercise 4 · The Hermes tool

Deploy Hermes Agent on your own computer, connect it to a chat app, direct it through one task, and let it distill the task into a skill.
Deliverable: a screenshot of the successful conversation + the resulting skill document.

Exercise 5 · Your own pick

Use any tool you learned today to solve a real problem from your own study or life. The one requirement: state the goal and acceptance criteria before you start, and afterwards review where the AI went wrong.

A hint: these exercises don't reward whoever finishes fastest, but whoever states it most clearly and verifies it most rigorously — question quality = output quality.
